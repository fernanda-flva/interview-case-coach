Analytical · Strategic
The Disappearing Revenue
Revenue Decomposition Hypothesis Generation Segment Analysis
"A mid-size UK retailer tells you their online revenue dropped 18% over the past 90 days. Their traffic is flat, their product catalog hasn't changed, and their marketing spend is actually up. You have access to transaction-level data: timestamp, product ID, customer ID, order value, payment method, device type, and region. Where do you start?"
Candidate — Opening Move: Reframe Before Analyzing
Before I touch the data, I want to make sure I understand what we're actually measuring. When you say revenue is down 18% — is that total revenue, or revenue per session? And is the drop driven by fewer orders, smaller orders, or both?

The reason I ask is that revenue = order volume × average order value, and these two levers have completely different root causes. A volume drop usually points to conversion or funnel issues. An AOV drop often points to product mix, discount behavior, or customers trading down. I'd want to know which one is doing the work before I build any hypothesis.

Also — when you say traffic is flat, is that sessions? Unique visitors? And is the traffic source mix stable, or are we seeing a shift from, say, organic to paid? Because if marketing spend is up but the traffic quality has changed — we're getting more people who are less likely to buy — that could explain a lot without any site-side problem at all.
Interviewer
Good questions. Let's say it's a mix — volume is down about 12%, AOV is down about 6%. Sessions are flat. The traffic is coming from mostly paid search, which has increased as a channel share over the past 90 days.
Candidate's Internal Reasoning (Surfaced Out Loud)
This is actually a significant clue. Paid search volume increasing as a share while organic stays flat means the customer intent profile may have shifted. Paid search attracts higher-intent but lower-loyalty buyers — they often have lower AOV because they're searching for specific items, not browsing. This could explain both the volume drop (paid has lower session quality) and the AOV drop (less basket-building). I'll flag this as my top hypothesis immediately.
Candidate — Hypothesis Structure
Okay, that paid search shift is really interesting. Let me lay out the framework I want to use to work through this, and then I'll rank my hypotheses.

I want to segment the problem across three dimensions first:
Traffic Quality
Has the mix of high-intent vs browse traffic changed? Paid vs organic vs direct shifts all carry different conversion expectations.
Funnel Behavior
Where in the funnel are we losing people? Product page? Cart? Payment? Each drop-off zone implies a different fix.
Transaction Structure
Which products, regions, devices, and payment methods are most affected? Concentration = a clue about the cause.
My ranked hypotheses, based on what you've told me:
Hypotheses — ranked by likelihood given the data shared
H1
Paid search traffic is lower quality, depressing both conversion rate and AOV. If paid visitors are searching for specific low-price items, they buy less and browse less. Test: compare conversion rate and AOV for paid vs organic sessions. If organic is holding and paid is down, this is confirmed.
H2
A checkout or payment friction point appeared in the last 90 days. Flat traffic with lower conversion usually means something broke in the funnel. Test: look at cart abandonment rate by device type. If mobile abandonment spiked specifically at the payment step, suspect a gateway or UX regression.
H3
Product mix shifted — high-AOV categories are being purchased less. Even with the same catalog, customers may be gravitating toward lower-price items due to external economic pressure or seasonality. Test: look at revenue by product category. If top-quartile SKUs lost share, this is it.
H4
A specific region or device segment has nearly stopped converting. If one region represents 30% of volume and dropped to near-zero, the overall 18% becomes explainable by a local event. Test: revenue by region × device over time, look for a single segment that fell off a cliff.
◎
Check-in: "Before I go deeper on any of these — does H1 resonate with what your team has been seeing, or is there a signal I'm not picking up on yet? I want to make sure I'm not running in the wrong direction."
Interviewer
The paid search quality angle is interesting but the business says their campaign targeting hasn't changed. Let's assume H1 is less likely. What's your next move?
Candidate — Drilling Into H2 and H3
Perfect — I'd pivot straight to the funnel analysis. From the transaction data I have, I can derive a crude conversion funnel by looking at timestamps: how many sessions resulted in a completed purchase vs. abandoned at various stages. I can also look at whether the abandonment pattern changed in the 90-day window.

Specifically, I'd pull:
cart_abandonment_rate by device payment_method success rate avg_order_value by product_category revenue by region × week repeat vs new customer mix
That last one — repeat vs new — matters because if we're suddenly acquiring fewer repeat customers via email/organic and replacing them with new paid visitors, repeat buyers tend to have 2–3x the AOV of first-time buyers. That alone could swing AOV by 6%.
🔷
Explicit assumption: I'm assuming the data has enough timestamp granularity to identify when in the checkout a session terminated. If we only have completed transactions, I can still see the AOV and mix story but not the funnel drop-off. I'd flag this data gap to the client early.
My recommendation, given all of this: I'd start with the product category mix analysis because it requires the least data engineering and could resolve the AOV question in under an hour. Then move to device-level funnel analysis if the mix story doesn't explain it.
Candidate — Recommendation & Recovery Plan
To close this out: regardless of which hypothesis wins, here's how I'd structure the recommendation to the client.
Immediate (Week 1)
Run the segmentation analysis — device, region, product category. Pin the fault zone. If it's a checkout issue, escalate to engineering immediately. This stops the bleeding.
Short-Term (Weeks 2–4)
Fix the structural cause. Rebalance paid vs organic budget if traffic quality is the issue. Run A/B test on checkout if it's friction. Shift product merchandising if it's category mix.
Ongoing Monitoring
Set up weekly alerts on AOV, conversion rate by device, and channel mix. Revenue at the total level is a lagging indicator — you want leading metrics that catch this in week one, not week 12.
Alternative Approaches & Why They're Weaker
AvoidStarting with the raw data before forming hypotheses
Some candidates open a dataset and start computing averages immediately. This produces outputs without direction — you'll find interesting facts but won't know if they're relevant to the problem. Always form hypotheses before querying.
AcceptableFocusing solely on conversion rate
Not wrong, but incomplete. Revenue = volume × AOV. Fixing conversion without addressing AOV decline only solves half the problem. A complete answer covers both drivers simultaneously.
Strong AlternativeCohort analysis — comparing the same customer segments over time
If you can identify returning customers vs new customers across the 90-day window, you can isolate whether the problem is acquisition quality (new customers buying less) or retention quality (existing customers coming back less). This is a more sophisticated cut that a great candidate might offer.

Operations · Analytical
The Overloaded Hospital Department
Process Stage Decomposition Bottleneck Analysis Supply vs Demand
"A hospital outpatient department has wait times averaging 47 minutes above their target. You're given: patient ID, arrival time, check-in time, appointment time, appointment type, clinician ID, room ID, and discharge time. Two hours to find the root cause and recommendations. Walk me through your approach."
Candidate — Opening: Stage Decomposition
The first thing I want to resist is treating "wait time" as a single number. 47 minutes above target is an outcome — the cause is hiding inside specific stages of the patient journey. So my starting point is to decompose the total visit time into stages using the columns I have.

From the data, I can construct this stage map:
Patient Journey Stages — derived from data columns
S1
Arrival → Check-in. arrival_time to check_in_time. This is administrative queue time. If it's long, suspect understaffing at reception or a high walk-in rate overwhelming scheduled intake.
S2
Check-in → Appointment Start. check_in_time to appointment_time. This is the waiting room phase — patients waiting to be called. Delays here suggest scheduling overload, late clinicians, or room unavailability.
S3
Appointment Start → Discharge. appointment_time to discharge_time. This is the active care phase. Delays here could mean appointment types are running long relative to their scheduled slots, or specific clinicians are slower.
Once I calculate the average and variance for each stage, I'll know which stage is absorbing most of the 47 extra minutes. High average duration = capacity problem. High variance = scheduling or complexity problem.
🔷
Explicit assumption out loud: I'm assuming "appointment time" is when the patient is first seen by a clinician, not just when a room was assigned. If those are different events, stage S2 and S3 would need to be split further — but I'd flag that and work with what I have.
◎
Check-in: "Does this stage breakdown match how the department actually operates, or is there a step in the flow I'm missing — like a triage stage or a pre-consultation intake?"
Interviewer
That's a reasonable breakdown. Let's say the data shows Stage 2 — check-in to appointment start — accounts for about 34 of the 47 extra minutes. Stage 3 is running slightly long too, but Stage 2 is the main story.
Candidate — Drilling Into Stage 2
Stage 2 being the bottleneck tells me we're dealing with a capacity or scheduling mismatch — patients are ready and checked in, but they can't be seen. Let me split this into demand-side and supply-side hypotheses.
Demand-Side Hypotheses
Volume is exceeding designed capacity:

• Walk-in patients arriving unscheduled and absorbing appointment slots
• Patient volume has grown but clinical capacity hasn't scaled
• No-show patterns causing overbooking that then materializes
Supply-Side Hypotheses
Clinical or room capacity isn't available when needed:

• Clinicians running late or starting sessions late
• Rooms being held by patients finishing Stage 3 late, blocking intake
• Specific appointment types booked incorrectly (short slots for long consults)
To distinguish these, I'd look at:
room_id utilization by hour clinician_id avg start latency appointment_type duration vs scheduled slot arrival pattern by hour of day stage 2 duration by day of week
If I see that rooms are idle during peak Stage 2 wait time, it's a clinician problem. If rooms are occupied, it's a room turnover problem. If both are available but waits are long, it's a scheduling design problem — appointments are being packed in without buffer.
Candidate — Recommendations
Let me give you a tiered set of recommendations, ordered by how quickly they can be implemented:
Recommendations — by implementation speed
R1
Schedule audit — this week. Pull the distribution of scheduled appointment durations vs actual (appointment_time to discharge_time). If specific appointment types are systematically running 15 minutes over their slots, you have a scheduling model problem that's compounding across the day. Fix the slot lengths.
R2
Walk-in intake policy — this week. If walk-ins represent more than 15-20% of volume, they're likely causing scheduling cascade failures. Introduce a capped walk-in window (e.g., first 30 minutes of each hour) or a dedicated walk-in track.
R3
Clinician start-time monitoring — within a month. If specific clinicians are systematically starting late, that's a management conversation, not a data conversation. But the data creates the business case. Build a simple per-clinician punctuality dashboard.
R4
Dynamic room allocation — longer term. If room utilization data shows idle rooms during peak wait periods, there's a room assignment logic issue. A simple rules-based allocation system (assign next available room automatically at check-in completion) could reduce Stage 2 time structurally.
◎
Check-in: "Before I wrap this up — are any of these recommendations already being tried, or is there a political constraint in the department I should factor into the prioritization? For example, if clinician monitoring is off the table, I'd lean harder into the scheduling model fix."
Alternative Approaches
AvoidRecommending "add more staff" without identifying the bottleneck stage
Adding staff is expensive and often wrong. If the bottleneck is scheduling model design, more staff won't help — the schedule will still overpack the rooms. Identify the stage before prescribing the fix.
Strong AlternativeSimulation modeling — "what if" scenarios on schedule changes
A strong candidate might suggest building a simple discrete-event simulation using the historical data to model what happens to Stage 2 wait times if appointment slots are extended by 5, 10, or 15 minutes. This quantifies the impact of fixes before committing to them — exactly the kind of deployable analysis a top consulting firm would pitch.

The One-Week Taxi Intelligence System
Product Scoping Constraint-Driven Design Data Derivation
"You're handed 8,000 records of London taxi data: trip ID, fare amount, pickup location, dropoff location, pickup time, dropoff time, distance, driver ID. A client wants a deployable product in one week that makes their operations smarter. Walk me through how you'd scope, design, and prioritize the solution."
Candidate — Immediately Challenging the Brief
I want to start by pushing back on "smarter" — not to be difficult, but because the word is doing a lot of work in this brief and the entire scope depends on what it actually means in practice.

There are at least four very different things this client could mean:
Driver Intelligence
Help drivers find the next fare faster. Reduce idle time. Show where demand is clustering by time of day.
Fleet Intelligence
Help dispatchers allocate vehicles optimally. Reduce deadhead miles. Improve coverage of high-demand zones.
Revenue Intelligence
Identify pricing anomalies. Find high-value routes being underserved. Understand fare efficiency by driver or route.
Performance Intelligence
Score drivers on efficiency metrics. Identify outliers. Create accountability and coaching opportunities.
Each of these is a different product with different outputs and different users. So before I touch the data — who is the end user of this system, and what decision do they make with it?
Interviewer
Let's say the client is primarily a fleet manager. They want to understand driver efficiency and which routes or time slots are most and least profitable. They don't have any existing analytics tool.
Candidate — What the Data Can Actually Tell Us
Perfect — now I can be specific. Let me map what I can derive from the 8 columns without needing any additional data:
Derivable Metrics — 8 columns → actionable signals
D1
Revenue per mile = fare ÷ distance. Immediately surfaces high-value vs low-value routes. A trip to Heathrow might look big but earn £1.20/mile. A short City run might earn £3.40/mile. This is the fleet manager's core efficiency metric.
D2
Trip duration = dropoff_time − pickup_time. Divide fare by duration to get revenue per hour. Identify which time windows are most lucrative. A 9pm Friday may 2x daytime rates.
D3
Driver efficiency score = driver's avg revenue/mile vs fleet average. With driver_id, I can rank all drivers. Outliers — high and low — are immediately identifiable for coaching or best practice sharing.
D4
Demand heatmap by pickup location × hour of day. From pickup_location and pickup_time, I can build an 8×24 grid (or finer) of where and when trips originate. This tells the fleet manager where to position vehicles during each time window.
D5
Fare anomaly detection. Trips with similar distance/duration but very different fares. Could indicate pricing inconsistency, metering errors, or route choices worth investigating.
🔷
Explicit data assumptions I'm making: Location fields are either named zones or coordinates I can cluster — not free-text strings. If they're postcodes, I can still group them. If they're named landmarks, I'll need a lookup table. I'd validate this in hour one before committing to the heatmap feature.
Candidate — One-Week Scope
Now the critical question: what can I actually build in a week? The one-week constraint forces a ruthless prioritization. My v1 scope would be:
In Scope — Week 1
✓ Revenue/mile and revenue/hour by route cluster
✓ Driver efficiency leaderboard with fleet benchmarks
✓ Peak demand time windows by location zone
✓ Simple dashboard with 3-4 charts (Foundry native)
✓ Anomaly flag for trips that are fare outliers
Out of Scope — Phase 2
✗ Live/real-time tracking integration
✗ Predictive demand forecasting
✗ Dynamic pricing engine
✗ Route optimization algorithm
✗ Driver app integration
The v1 gives the fleet manager four actionable decisions they can make on Monday morning: which zones to position vehicles in, which drivers to coach, which time slots to target, and which routes to prioritize. That's a successful one-week product.
◎
Check-in: "Does this scope feel right given the one-week constraint? I've deliberately left out predictive features because those require model validation time we don't have. If the client is expecting a forecasting tool, I'd want to reset expectations now rather than in week three."
Candidate — System Architecture (Non-Technical)
Even without coding, I'd sketch three layers:
Layer 1 — Data
Ingest the 8,000 records. Clean location fields. Derive D1-D5 metrics. Flag nulls and outliers. Deduplicate on trip_id. This is Day 1-2 work.
Layer 2 — Logic
Compute aggregated metrics: driver rankings, zone demand tables, hourly revenue profiles. These are table transforms — no ML required. Day 3-4.
Layer 3 — Output
A dashboard with 4 views: zone heatmap, driver leaderboard, time-slot revenue curve, fare anomaly list. Day 5-7, including client walkthrough.
Alternative Approaches
AvoidProposing an ML model as the core output
8,000 records is not enough to train a robust predictive model, and the one-week timeline doesn't allow for validation cycles. Candidates who jump to "I'd build a demand prediction model" are proposing a product that can't be safely delivered.
AcceptableAPI-first design
Some candidates will propose building a REST API that serves the derived metrics to a frontend. This is valid but over-engineered for week one — a dashboard is faster to build and more immediately useful to a fleet manager who doesn't have an existing tech team to consume an API.
Strong AlternativeStart with one single insight, made extremely actionable
A great candidate might argue: "Rather than four charts, I'd rather deliver one thing the fleet manager will definitely use — the demand heatmap — and make it exceptional. Then add the other views in week two based on what they actually found useful." This shows product maturity over feature-maximalism.

The Warehouse That Can't Keep Up
Bottleneck Isolation Temporal Analysis Intervention Prioritization
"On-time dispatch rates dropped from 94% to 78% over 6 months. No obvious cause. Data: order ID, order entry time, pick start time, pack start time, dispatch time, SKU count, picker ID, packer ID, dock door. Where do you begin?"
Candidate — The 6-Month Timeline Is the First Clue
The first thing that jumps out is the six-month window. A 16-percentage-point drop in dispatch performance over six months is not random variance — something structurally changed. So before I build any analysis, I want to find the inflection point: when exactly did the decline start? Was it a step change on a specific day, or a gradual drift?

I'd plot on-time dispatch rate by week for the full six months. The shape of the curve tells me a lot:
Sudden Drop on Specific Date
Suggests a discrete event — a system change, policy shift, a key person leaving, or a new client adding complex orders suddenly.
Gradual Drift Downward
Suggests a structural load problem — volume growing without capacity scaling, or order complexity increasing incrementally (more SKUs per order).
Step Down Then Plateau
Classic process change adoption problem — a new system was introduced, performance dropped, and the team never fully recovered. Think WMS upgrades or picking methodology changes.
Candidate — Stage Decomposition
In parallel to the timeline analysis, I'd decompose the warehouse process using the data I have:
Process Stage Times — derived from timestamps
S1
Entry → Pick Start: queue time before picking begins. Long here = order backlog, insufficient pickers, or poor workload allocation.
S2
Pick Start → Pack Start: picking duration. Long here = complex orders (high SKU count), poor pick path, or picker performance variance.
S3
Pack Start → Dispatch: packing + dock allocation. Long here = packing complexity, dock door congestion, or carrier collection timing misalignment.
I'd calculate mean and standard deviation of each stage, then look at how these have changed across the six-month window. The stage with the highest increase in variance is usually where the problem lives.
◎
Check-in: "Before I go deeper — has anything obvious changed operationally in the last six months that the ops team knows about? New WMS, new client, staff restructure? I want to make sure my analysis isn't re-discovering something already known."
Interviewer
The team thinks it might be related to a new large client they onboarded 5 months ago, but they haven't been able to confirm it. Let's say your stage analysis shows that Stage 2 — picking — has become significantly longer on average, and the variance has increased a lot.
Candidate — Testing the New Client Hypothesis
That really does point to the new client. Here's the logic: a new large client often brings either high SKU count orders (complex picks that take longer) or high volume concentration (large batches arriving at once, overwhelming picking capacity during specific windows).

I'd test this concretely by:
SKU count distribution — before and after month 5 pick_duration by SKU_count quartile picker_id throughput by month order arrival time pattern — is there a volume spike? on-time rate segmented by order type or client
My working hypothesis: the new client is sending orders with significantly more SKUs per order, which the picking process wasn't designed for. The old pick path design assumed, say, 3-4 SKUs per order. If the new client averages 12-15, pick times are 3-4x longer per order, and the whole flow backs up.
🔷
Assumption flagged: I don't have a client ID field in the data. I'd need to either join a separate client/order table, or use order entry time patterns to infer which orders are likely from the new client (e.g., they may arrive in a consistent time window or have a different SKU count signature).
Candidate — Recommendations
Assuming the new client / high SKU order hypothesis holds, I'd recommend three interventions:
Interventions — ordered by speed and impact
R1
Dedicated picking lane for high-SKU orders — this week. Separate complex orders (say, 8+ SKUs) into a dedicated pick stream with specialized pickers. This stops them from blocking the standard-flow queue and immediately protects dispatch rate for the other 80% of orders.
R2
Slot time renegotiation with the new client — this month. If they're sending large orders with same-day dispatch expectations, their SLA may be commercially misaligned with the warehouse's capability. This is a business conversation enabled by the data — "here's what your order profile is doing to our dispatch rate."
R3
Pick path optimization for high-SKU orders — next quarter. A structural solution: re-engineer the pick path layout and sequence logic for orders above a SKU threshold. This requires WMS changes but is the permanent fix.
Alternative Approaches
AvoidRecommending more staff without doing the stage analysis
If the problem is pick path design or order complexity, more staff running the same broken process just costs more money for marginal gains. Stage analysis must come before staffing decisions.
Strong AlternativeDock door analysis as a secondary bottleneck
A strong candidate would also look at dock door utilization — if three dock doors are allocated to carrier A and carrier A collects at 4pm, but orders are ready at 2pm, there's a 2-hour idle buffer being wasted. Dock scheduling optimization can be a quick win even if it's not the primary cause.

The Feature Nobody Uses
Product Strategy Adoption Analysis Kill vs Invest Decision
"A SaaS company launched a collaboration feature 3 months ago. Expected 40% adoption in 90 days; actual is 9%. Leadership is debating kill vs invest. Data: user ID, company ID, account tier, days since signup, logins in last 30 days, feature used (Y/N), usage count. What do you recommend?"
Candidate — First Move: Challenge the 40% Benchmark
I want to start by questioning the benchmark, because the entire framing of this conversation rests on 40% being the right target. Where did that number come from?

If it came from user research — interviews with the target segment about intended behavior — then 9% is a meaningful signal that something is wrong. But if it was an aspirational number set by a product manager or executive without empirical grounding, then 9% might actually be completely normal for a collaboration feature in this category. B2B collaboration features typically see 10-20% adoption in the first 90 days because they require multiple people in an organization to change behavior simultaneously, which is inherently slower than individual feature adoption.

So my first question is: how was 40% derived, and what's the comparable benchmark for similar features in this product category?
Interviewer
Fair point. Let's say the 40% was internally set — it wasn't based on external benchmarks. The product team felt it was achievable. What does that change?
Candidate — Reframing the Question
It changes the framing significantly. Now the question isn't "why did we miss 40%" — it's "is 9% actually a problem?" To answer that, I need to diagnose what type of failure this is, because there are three fundamentally different problems that all look like low adoption:
Discovery Failure
Users never found the feature. It exists but isn't surfaced in the right moments in the product experience. Fix: onboarding, in-app prompts, email campaigns.
Value Failure
Users found it, tried it, and decided it wasn't worth using again. The feature doesn't solve a real problem or solves it worse than alternatives. Fix: redesign or kill.
Fit Failure
The feature is actually good, but it was built for the wrong segment. Enterprise users might love it, SMB users may never need it. Fix: reposition, not rebuild.
The kill vs invest decision is completely different for each of these. You don't kill a discovery failure — you fix the surface. You don't invest more in a value failure — you kill or fundamentally redesign. So I need to diagnose which type this is before I give any recommendation.
Candidate — Using the Data to Diagnose
From the data I have, here's exactly how I'd distinguish the three failure types:
Diagnostic Tests — using the available columns
T1
Usage count among the 9% who adopted. If the adopters used it 8, 12, 15 times — they love it. This is a discovery failure: the feature has real value, it just isn't reaching most users. If most used it once and stopped, it's a value failure.
T2
Account tier of adopters vs non-adopters. If adoption is 35% in enterprise accounts and 3% in SMB, this is a fit failure. The feature works exactly where it was supposed to — it just wasn't designed for the majority of your user base.
T3
Logins in last 30 days for adopters vs non-adopters. If non-adopters are highly active users with many logins, it's unlikely they never encountered the feature — which makes it a value or fit failure rather than discovery. If non-adopters are low-engagement users overall, the feature may just be hard to surface to people who aren't power users.
T4
Days since signup for adopters. If most adopters signed up recently and the feature was prominently featured in new user onboarding, that tells me the feature IS discoverable for new users — but existing users never got the same exposure. The fix is a re-engagement campaign, not a product change.
◎
Check-in: "Based on these four tests — do you have any initial signal about which failure type is most likely? Even an informal impression from the product team would help me prioritize which test to run first."
Candidate — Recommendation Framework
My recommendation depends on what the tests show, but here's the decision tree I'd use:
Kill vs Invest Decision Framework
→
High repeat usage in the 9% → Invest in discovery. Don't touch the feature. Fix the surface: better onboarding tooltip, email campaign to non-adopters, in-app banner. Budget: low. Speed: fast.
→
Adoption concentrated in enterprise only → Reposition. Change the product marketing, not the feature. Move it from "for everyone" to "for teams over 10 users." Set the right expectation about who it's for. Don't kill it and don't rebuild it.
→
Low repeat usage, no segment concentration → Investigate value. Talk to the 9%. Why did they use it? Why did they stop? If the feedback is "it didn't solve my problem," you have a value failure. Then: kill or major redesign based on cost-benefit.
→
No clear pattern → Don't make a permanent decision yet. Run a 30-day targeted campaign to a specific segment and measure response. Kill or invest based on that result, not on the ambiguous baseline.
I would strongly advise leadership against making a kill decision without this diagnosis. Killing a discovery-failure feature is destroying value you already built. But investing more in a value-failure feature is burning money. The data makes this call — not intuition.
Alternative Approaches
AvoidRecommending kill or invest immediately without the diagnostic
Both decisions are binary and irreversible in their own way. Kill = sunk cost fully written off. Invest = more burn on potentially a wrong direction. Never make these calls without the failure type diagnosis.
Strong AlternativeRetention as the primary metric, not adoption
A sophisticated product thinker would reframe the entire question: "9% adoption is less important than the retention rate of that 9%. If those users are still active and using the feature 90 days later, you have a strong core. Most successful features start at 5-10% and grow through network effects and word of mouth." This reframe can completely change the recommendation.

The Client Who Wants Everything
Scope Management Client Communication Trade-off Framing
"Three weeks into a consulting engagement. The client is an energy company. Original scope: procurement analytics dashboard. Now requesting: supplier risk scoring, invoice reconciliation automation, and vendor onboarding workflow — all within the original four-week timeline. Your technical lead says the original scope is already tight. How do you handle this?"
Candidate — First Move: Understand the Why Behind Each Request
My first instinct is not to say yes and not to say no. Both of those responses would be premature. I need to understand what's driving each of the four new requests, because scope creep in client engagements almost always has a signal buried in it.

There are usually three reasons a client expands scope mid-engagement:
Expanding Confidence
They see the work we're doing and now trust us with more. The relationship is accelerating faster than the contract. This is a growth opportunity, not a problem.
Shifting Priorities
An internal event (audit, board review, new VP) changed what matters. The original scope is now less urgent than new items. We need to understand what's really highest priority.
Original Scope Was Wrong
The discovery wasn't thorough enough. We scoped the dashboard but didn't surface the reconciliation and vendor problems that are actually the bigger pain. This is partly on us.
Before I do anything else, I'd have a 30-minute conversation with the client sponsor — not a big meeting, just a direct conversation — and ask: "Walk me through what's driving each of these requests. What happens if we don't deliver them?"
Interviewer
Good. Let's say the client says their procurement director just got a new mandate from the CFO around supplier risk following a supply chain disruption. That's driving the risk scoring request urgently. The invoice reconciliation and onboarding requests are more opportunistic — "since you're here."
Candidate — Using Data to Structure the Trade-Off
Now I have what I need to have a productive conversation. The supplier risk scoring is genuinely urgent — it has a direct business driver and a CFO mandate. The other two are opportunistic. That changes everything about how I respond.

Here's the trade-off framework I'd present to the client, using the data from our current engagement:
Scope Trade-Off — presented transparently to the client
S1
Option A: Original scope only, delivered with full quality. Procurement analytics dashboard live in week four as agreed. All four new items deferred to a Phase 2 proposal. Risk: client may feel we're not responsive to the CFO mandate.
S2
Option B: Swap dashboard for supplier risk scoring as the primary deliverable. If supplier risk is now the CFO priority, we could deprioritize the dashboard and deliver a v1 risk scoring model instead. Dashboard becomes Phase 2. This only works if the original stakeholders agree to the swap.
S3
Option C: Scope both, but at reduced depth on each. We deliver a dashboard plus a basic supplier risk tier (high/medium/low), but neither is as polished as the original scope promised. Risk: we deliver two mediocre things instead of one excellent thing. I'd advise against this unless the client explicitly accepts the quality trade-off in writing.
S4

I would present all four options to the client with their trade-offs clearly stated, let them choose, and document the decision. My personal recommendation would be Option A or B — not Option C, because shipping two half-finished things is a worse outcome for the relationship than shipping one great thing.
🔷
Explicit assumption: I'm assuming the invoice reconciliation and vendor onboarding requests are not connected to a regulatory or compliance deadline. If they are, they move up the urgency ranking immediately. I'd ask the client that directly before finalizing my recommendation.
Candidate — Managing the Internal Team
One dimension I haven't addressed yet: my technical lead. This isn't only a client conversation — it's also an internal one. I'd loop my technical lead in before the client meeting with three specific asks:

First, give me a concrete estimate: if we replaced the dashboard with supplier risk scoring, could we deliver a v1 in the same timeline? What's the delta in effort? I need real numbers, not a vague "it's tight."

Second, what's the minimum viable version of the risk scoring that would satisfy a CFO mandate? A full ML model? No. A supplier tier classification based on existing procurement data — volume, on-time delivery, contract age, geographic concentration? Probably yes, in a week.

Recurring Themes & Transferable Frameworks
Six cases, one mental model — and twelve cases, twelve variations. Every decomposition question in this type of interview tests some combination of these patterns. Master these and you can walk into any scenario cold.
1. Decompose Before Diagnosing
Q1 Q2 Q4
The single most consistent pattern across all six cases: never form hypotheses on the aggregate metric. Revenue is down → decompose into volume × AOV. Wait time is up → decompose into S1, S2, S3 stage times. Dispatch rate fell → decompose into entry→pick→pack→dispatch.

The decomposition tells you where the problem lives. Without it, any hypothesis is a guess. With it, hypotheses are constrained by the data and far more likely to be right.
Framework: Metric → Components → Fault Component → Hypotheses → Tests
2. Challenge the Premise Before Accepting It
Q3 Q5 Q6
In Q3, "smarter operations" needed to be unpacked into a specific user and decision. In Q5, the 40% adoption benchmark needed to be questioned. In Q6, "add more scope" needed to be traced back to the underlying business driver.

Accepting the brief at face value is the most common mistake. Great candidates spend 60-90 seconds reframing the question before touching any data. This isn't stalling — it's the highest-value thing you can do in a decomposition interview because it proves you won't solve the wrong problem elegantly.
Question to ask yourself: "What assumption is baked into this prompt that I should surface?"
3. Supply vs Demand as a Universal Split
Q2 Q4 Q6
Almost every operational problem can be split into: is this a demand problem (more is coming in than expected) or a supply problem (less capacity to handle it than before)?

Hospital waits: demand spike (more patients) vs supply drop (fewer available clinicians/rooms). Warehouse delays: demand increase (new high-SKU client) vs supply constraint (picking process not designed for complexity). Scope creep: demand expansion (client confidence growing) vs supply constraint (team at capacity).

This split immediately narrows your hypotheses in half and points to completely different interventions.
Always ask: "Did demand increase, did supply decrease, or did both shift simultaneously?"
4. The Inflection Point Test
Q1 Q4
When a metric has degraded over time, the shape of the degradation curve is itself a diagnostic signal. A sudden cliff drop on a specific date points to a discrete event (a technical change, a policy shift, a person leaving). A gradual drift points to a structural load problem (volume creeping up, complexity slowly increasing). A step-down-then-plateau suggests a process change whose effects were never fully recovered.

This applies to any time-series problem: customer churn rates, revenue trends, NPS scores, SLA breach rates. Always plot the metric over time before doing any segment analysis — the curve shape gives you the answer to "what changed?" before you even look at the data.
Plot the trend first. The curve shape is a hypothesis generator, not just a visualization.
5. Constraint-Driven Scoping
Q3 Q6
In consulting engagements, the constraint is almost always time — one week, four weeks, a client engagement window. Strong candidates internalize this and scope to the constraint from the start, rather than proposing an ideal solution and then reluctantly paring it back.

The discipline of "what's the minimum useful output within the constraint?" produces better client outcomes than "here's what we could build if we had unlimited time." A focused, excellent v1 builds more trust than an overscoped mediocre v1.
Lead with the constraint: "Given one week, here's what I'd prioritize and here's what I'd explicitly defer."
6. Make Assumptions Explicit and Early
Q1 Q2 Q3 Q4 Q5 Q6
Every case required assumptions — about data granularity, about what fields mean, about who the end user is, about what the benchmark was based on. The difference between a strong and a weak candidate is not whether they make assumptions (everyone does) — it's whether they surface them out loud and invite correction.

Making an assumption explicit does three things: it shows you know what you don't know, it gives the interviewer a chance to redirect you before you waste time on a wrong path, and it demonstrates intellectual honesty — a core trait this type of interview explicitly evaluates.
For every assumption you make, say it out loud: "I'm assuming X — is that valid, or should I account for Y?"
7. Check In Strategically, Not Constantly
Q1 Q2 Q3 Q4 Q5 Q6
There are three moments in every case where a check-in creates maximum value: after the initial decomposition (confirm you've framed the problem correctly), after forming hypotheses (confirm you're not missing a known driver), and before committing to a final recommendation (confirm there are no constraints that would invalidate your answer).

Checking in more often than this signals anxiety or lack of direction. Checking in less often signals arrogance or poor client management. The right cadence shows you're confident enough to drive but humble enough to course-correct.
Three check-in moments: after decomposition, after hypotheses, before recommendation.
8. Characterize Before You Hypothesize
Q7 Q10
For anomaly and quality cases, the pattern of the problem is itself a diagnostic signal. Before forming any hypothesis about why something is happening, spend 60 seconds asking where it is happening — which machines, which zones, which shifts, which batches. Concentration analysis before causal reasoning is the discipline that separates analysts from guessers.
Find where the failures cluster before building any story about why they happen.
9. Separate Model Failure From Deployment Failure
Q8 Q12
Any time an AI system or deployed tool is underperforming, the first question is: is the underlying product broken, or is the way it was deployed broken? These are completely different fixes. A model with wrong predictions needs retraining or rollback. A correctly-performing model deployed to the wrong population, at the wrong threshold, or without change management, needs a deployment fix — not a product fix. Conflating these is the most expensive mistake in technology delivery.
Ask: "Is the thing broken, or is the way we're using the thing broken?"
10. Assess Data Fitness Before Using It
Q11
When you're handed data to inform a decision, the first question is never "what does this data say?" — it's "is this data reliable enough to base this decision on?" Age, completeness, source, and context all affect fitness. 18-month-old healthcare data in a fast-changing regulatory environment may be worse than no data at all if it creates false confidence. A strong candidate challenges data fitness before building any model on it.
Before modeling: "Is this data fit for this decision, or are we building precision on a shaky foundation?"
11. Containment and Root Cause Run in Parallel
Q7 Q8 Q10
On safety-critical or financially impactful cases, never wait for complete root cause analysis before taking any action. "We need more data" is not an acceptable answer when turbines are failing, loans are defaulting at 40% higher rates, or components are shipping at 81% yield. The professional move is to run an immediate containment action — tighten a threshold, quarantine a batch, increase inspection frequency — while root cause work runs in parallel.
Ask: "What can I do today to limit harm, before I know exactly what caused it?"
12. Own the Outcome, Not Just the Delivery
Q6 Q12
This type of interview explicitly evaluates ownership — and Q12 tests it directly. When a tool you deployed isn't getting adopted, the weak response is to defend the delivery ("we built what was scoped") or concede failure ("we're sorry"). The strong response is to own the gap and immediately present a recovery plan. Deployment is not the finish line. Value realization is the finish line. This mindset — "my job isn't done until the client is getting value" — is the single most important signal this interview is looking for.
"Our job isn't done at deployment. It's done when your team is getting value."
