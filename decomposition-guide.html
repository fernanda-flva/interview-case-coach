Problem Decomposition Interview
Senior Business Operations & Strategy
Interviewer Reference Guide
Interview Purpose
Evaluate how candidates structure open-ended, ambiguous problems using data-driven reasoning — not technical knowledge or coding ability.
Duration & Format
45–60 minutes. Conversational. One scenario presented verbally; interviewer plays the "client." Candidate drives the dialogue.
What We're Screening For
Smart + Want to Win: analytical thinking, structured creativity, ownership mentality, and tolerance for ambiguity under time pressure.
Tags Analytical Strategic Operations Product
Problem Solving
Empirical, evidence-based. Can they break a mess into components?
Creativity
Do they challenge the obvious framing? Generate non-obvious hypotheses?
Ownership
Do they drive the conversation, or wait to be led? Do they commit to a recommendation?
Chaos Tolerance
Do they stay calm and structured under ambiguity? Can they adapt mid-conversation?

Analytical Strategic
The Disappearing Revenue
"A mid-size UK retailer tells you their online revenue dropped 18% over the past 90 days. Their traffic is flat, their product catalog hasn't changed, and their marketing spend is actually up. They want to know what happened and what to do. You have access to their transaction-level data: timestamp, product ID, customer ID, order value, payment method, device type, and region. Where do you start?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Clarify the metric. Is it revenue decline or conversion decline? Is it volume or average order value? Strong candidates distinguish these immediately.
2
Segment before diagnosing. They should want to slice by region, device, payment method, and product category to isolate where the drop is concentrated — not treat it as uniform.
3
Build hypotheses from the segments. e.g., "If mobile is the culprit, I'd suspect a checkout UX change or payment gateway issue." They should generate 3–5 specific hypotheses ranked by testability.
4
Identify what data confirms or kills each hypothesis. e.g., "Look at cart abandonment rate by device — if mobile abandonment spiked at payment step, that's the fault zone."
5
Recommend a decision, not just an analysis. They should end with a prioritized action: what to fix first, how to measure recovery, and what to monitor going forward.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Is the decline uniform across all products? Any recent site or checkout changes? Is cart abandonment rate up? Any competitor pricing shifts? Which device types show the drop? Has average order value changed? Any regional clustering?
Assumptions They Should Explicitly State
Traffic quality may have changed even if volume hasn't Revenue = volume × AOV, both need checking 90-day window may mask a sudden vs gradual shift Marketing mix shift may have changed customer intent
Check-in Cadence
After segmentation step: "Does this breakdown match what you'd expect?" — After hypotheses: "Am I missing a major driver you'd want me to consider?" — Before final recommendation: "Is there a constraint I should factor in before I give you a priority?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Jumps to a single cause without segmenting
Can't distinguish between revenue, conversion, and AOV
Proposes more data collection without forming hypotheses
Ends with analysis but no recommendation
Strong Signals
Immediately segments before theorizing
Generates multiple falsifiable hypotheses
Links each hypothesis to a specific data test
Offers a prioritized recovery plan, not just a diagnosis

Operations Analytical
The Overloaded Hospital Department
"A hospital has flagged that one of its outpatient departments has unusually long wait times — averaging 47 minutes above their target. You're given anonymized visit-level data: patient ID, arrival time, check-in time, appointment time, appointment type, clinician ID, room ID, and discharge time. You have two hours to identify the root cause and a set of actionable recommendations. Walk me through your approach."
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Decompose the wait time into stages. Arrival → check-in, check-in → room assignment, room → clinician, clinician → discharge. Which stage is absorbing the excess 47 minutes?
2
Separate demand-side vs supply-side hypotheses. Demand: volume spikes, no-show patterns, walk-in vs appointment mix. Supply: clinician utilization, room bottlenecks, appointment type complexity.
3
Look for temporal and structural clustering. Are delays worse on specific days, times, or with specific clinicians? A staffing vs scheduling problem shows different patterns than a room capacity problem.
4
Model the impact of proposed interventions. If walk-in patients are 30% of volume but cause 70% of delay, capping walk-ins is a higher-leverage fix than adding rooms.
5
Rank recommendations by impact and ease of implementation. Consider what can be deployed in one week vs what requires a longer process change.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
What's the target wait time vs actual? Is this department-wide or specific appointment types? Has volume grown recently? Is there a scheduling system already in place? Are walk-ins vs booked appointments tracked? How many clinicians are on duty vs capacity?
Check-in Cadence
After stage decomposition: "Does this breakdown feel right, or is there a stage I'm missing?" — After hypotheses: "Which of these would be most surprising if it turned out to be true?" — After recommendations: "Is there a political or resource constraint that would block any of these?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Treats wait time as a single metric without decomposing stages
Only looks at one variable (e.g., staff count)
Recommendations not tied to data findings
Doesn't consider feasibility of interventions
Strong Signals
Naturally decomposes wait time into stages
Distinguishes demand-side vs supply-side root causes
Quantifies expected impact of recommendations
Considers implementation constraints proactively

Product Strategic
The One-Week Taxi Intelligence System
"You're handed 8,000 records of London taxi data with eight columns: trip ID, fare amount, pickup location, dropoff location, pickup time, dropoff time, distance, and driver ID. A client wants a deployable product built in one week that makes their taxi operations smarter. You don't need to code anything right now — walk me through how you'd scope, design, and prioritize the solution."
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Define "smarter" before designing anything. Is the client trying to maximize revenue per trip? Reduce dead miles? Improve driver allocation? A strong candidate rejects vague briefs and anchors to a specific objective.
2
Explore what the data can actually tell you. From these 8 columns: fare per mile efficiency, peak demand zones by hour/day, driver performance vs avg, high-value route clusters, and pricing anomalies are all derivable.
3
Scope ruthlessly for a one-week build. What's the minimum useful output? E.g., a dashboard showing demand heatmaps by time-of-day + driver efficiency scores is deployable in a week. A full dynamic pricing engine is not.
4
Name the components without over-engineering. Data ingestion → processing layer (derive speed, efficiency metrics) → visualization/output layer (Foundry dashboard or simple API). They should sketch this, not just describe it.
5
Identify risks and what they'd validate first. Data quality assumptions (e.g., are coordinates clean?), user adoption of output, and whether the insight actually changes behavior.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
What decision does the client want to make with this? Who is the end user — dispatcher, driver, exec? Is this data historical or does it update live? Are there existing systems this needs to integrate with? What does success look like at end of week one? Are location fields lat/long or named zones?
Check-in Cadence
After defining objective: "That's the direction I'd take this — does that align with what the business actually needs?" — After scoping: "Given that constraint, does a one-week v1 feel realistic, or should I adjust scope further?" — After architecture sketch: "Is there a part of this you'd want to go deeper on?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Jumps to building ML models without clarifying the problem
Can't distinguish what's derivable from the 8 columns
Doesn't scope for the one-week constraint
Proposes components without a user or decision in mind
Strong Signals
Reframes the brief before touching the data
Identifies what the 8 columns can/can't support
Scopes to a deliverable that fits the timeline
Considers end-user adoption, not just technical output

Strategic Operations
The Warehouse That Can't Keep Up
"A logistics company tells you their flagship warehouse has seen on-time dispatch rates fall from 94% to 78% over the past 6 months, with no obvious cause. You're given data on: order ID, order entry time, pick start time, pack start time, dispatch time, SKU count per order, picker ID, packer ID, and dock door used. Where do you begin and what do you expect to find?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Map the process stages from the data. Order entry → pick → pack → dispatch. Calculate time-in-stage for each order. The bottleneck will show as the stage with highest variance or longest average duration.
2
Look for the 6-month inflection point. Did order volume increase? Did SKU complexity increase? Did turnover in pickers/packers happen? The timing is a clue — something changed.
3
Test throughput vs complexity hypotheses. Are orders with higher SKU counts disproportionately late? Is a specific picker causing downstream delays? Are certain dock doors creating queues?
4
Build an intervention priority matrix. Quick wins (schedule optimization, dock door reassignment) vs structural fixes (training, layout change). Separate what's fixable in days vs months.
5
Define success metrics and monitoring. Don't just fix and leave — tell the client what to track (stage time per order, per-picker throughput) and at what threshold to escalate.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Has order volume or complexity changed in 6 months? Any staff turnover in the picking/packing teams? Are dispatch time targets fixed or variable by order type? Is the 78% uniform across shifts or worse at specific times? Are there seasonal patterns in the data? How many dock doors are available vs in use?
Check-in Cadence
After stage mapping: "Before I go deeper — is there a stage you already suspect is the problem?" — After bottleneck analysis: "Does the pattern I'm describing match what the ops team is experiencing on the floor?" — After recommendations: "What would change first if I'm right about the cause?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Treats dispatch rate as the only metric
Doesn't use the 6-month timeline as a diagnostic anchor
Suggests solutions without identifying the bottleneck stage
Ignores person-level data (pickers, packers) entirely
Strong Signals
Immediately maps data columns to process stages
Uses 6-month inflection as a key diagnostic signal
Tests both structural and behavioral hypotheses
Separates quick wins from structural changes

Product Analytical Strategic
The Feature Nobody Uses
"A SaaS company launched a new collaboration feature 3 months ago. The product team expected 40% of users to adopt it within 90 days; actual adoption is at 9%. Leadership is debating whether to kill the feature or invest in fixing it. You're given user-level data: user ID, company ID, account tier, days since signup, number of logins in last 30 days, whether the feature was used, and if so, how many times. What would you recommend and how would you get there?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Challenge the 40% target first. Was it evidence-based or a guess? If it was aspirational, 9% may not be a failure — it may be the product finding its right-fit segment.
2
Profile adopters vs non-adopters. Do the 9% cluster in specific account tiers, company sizes, or tenure bands? If adoption is high in enterprise accounts and low in SMB, the feature may be working exactly as it should for its target.
3
Separate discovery failures from value failures. Low adoption could mean users never found the feature, or that they found it and decided it wasn't valuable. These require completely different fixes.
4
Look at retention among adopters. If the 9% who used it used it repeatedly, that's a discovery problem, not a value problem — and the fix is surface/onboarding, not the feature itself.
5
Frame the kill vs invest decision clearly. Kill if: no adoption in target segment, one-time use with no repeat, qualitative signals of confusion. Invest if: strong retention in adopters, natural segment fit, fixable awareness gap.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
How was the 40% target derived? Is adoption consistent across account tiers? Was there an in-app announcement at launch? Are the 9% who adopted churning or retaining? What's the cost to maintain vs kill the feature? Any qualitative feedback from adopters vs non-adopters?
Check-in Cadence
After challenging the target: "Am I right to question the 40% baseline, or is there evidence it was grounded in user research?" — After profiling: "Do the adopter characteristics match the original target user persona?" — Before final recommendation: "Is there a timeline pressure on this decision I should factor in?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Accepts 40% as a valid benchmark without questioning it
Recommends killing or investing without segmenting adopters
Conflates discovery failures with product value failures
Doesn't use retention data as a signal
Strong Signals
Immediately challenges the benchmark's origin
Profiles adopters vs non-adopters before deciding
Distinguishes discovery from value failure
Uses repeat usage as a key signal for the invest/kill call

Strategic Operations Product
The Client Who Wants Everything
"You're three weeks into a consulting engagement. The client is a mid-size energy company. They initially asked for a procurement analytics dashboard. Now they're asking you to also build a supplier risk scoring model, automate their invoice reconciliation, and integrate a new vendor onboarding workflow — all within the original four-week timeline. Your technical lead says the original scope is already tight. How do you handle this, and how do you use data to have that conversation?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Separate the business need from the feature list. What underlying problem is the client trying to solve with each new request? Often scope creep is a symptom of unclear original success criteria.
2
Quantify the impact of each request. Which of the four items drives the most business value? Can that be determined from the data already collected — e.g., if invoice errors are causing measurable financial loss, reconciliation automation may outrank the dashboard.
3
Build a visible trade-off, not a refusal. "Here's what we can deliver in four weeks at current scope, and here's what gets dropped or de-scoped if we add X. Which trade-off do you want to make?" — This is a client conversation, not a unilateral decision.
4
Propose a phased roadmap. Deliver the original scope with high quality, then pitch phase 2 as a follow-on engagement. This protects delivery quality while keeping the relationship and opportunity alive.
5
Use data to anchor the conversation. Show the client the current build progress, the risk of quality degradation if scope expands, and the concrete value of the original deliverable on its own.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
What's driving the new requests — a specific event or pain? Has the client's success criteria changed? Is there budget or timeline flexibility on their side? Which of the four items is most urgent to the business? What happens if we deliver only the original scope excellently? Who is championing the new requests internally?
Check-in Cadence
This question tests how they handle internal + client dynamics simultaneously. Probe: "How would you bring your technical lead into this conversation?" and "How would you frame the trade-off to someone who doesn't read data?" Strong candidates close the loop on both relationships.
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Immediately says "no" without exploring the business need
Agrees to everything to avoid conflict
Doesn't use data to structure the trade-off conversation
Treats it as purely a project management problem, not a client relationship problem
Strong Signals
Separates the need behind each request before responding
Quantifies impact of each item to drive prioritization
Presents trade-offs visibly without being paternalistic
Protects team delivery quality while keeping client trust

Analytical Operations
The Energy Grid Anomaly
"A renewable energy operator has seen 14 unplanned grid outages in the past 6 months — up from 2 in the prior period. You have sensor data per turbine: timestamp, turbine ID, output (kWh), wind speed, temperature, maintenance flag, and operator ID. Two hours to find the pattern and recommend action."
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Characterize the outages before hypothesizing. Are they clustered in time (specific days/shifts), in equipment (specific turbine IDs), in conditions (high wind, low temp), or in operator coverage? The pattern type determines the root cause family.
2
Apply the inflection point test. When exactly did the rate jump from 2 to 14? A step change on a specific date suggests a discrete trigger — a software update, a new operator, a maintenance schedule change. A gradual increase suggests cumulative equipment degradation.
3
Separate equipment vs human vs environmental hypotheses. Equipment: specific turbines failing (maintenance_flag correlation). Human: specific operator_ids on shift during outages. Environmental: temperature or wind speed outside operating range at outage timestamps.
4
Recommend under uncertainty. Safety-critical infrastructure can't wait for perfect data. If three turbines account for 11 of 14 outages, recommend immediate preventive maintenance on those units while full analysis runs. Don't let perfect analysis block necessary action.
5
Build a monitoring threshold. Define the alert condition — e.g., any turbine with output variance >30% vs 30-day baseline triggers an inspection. The deliverable should be a live monitoring dashboard, not a one-time report.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Are outages concentrated on specific turbines or spread across all? Did anything change operationally 6 months ago? Is maintenance scheduled or reactive? What's the cost per outage — financial and reputational? Are environmental conditions logged at outage time? What's the acceptable risk threshold before shutting down a turbine?
Check-in Cadence
After outage characterization: "Do these patterns match what the ops team has observed, or am I missing a known factor?" — Before recommendation: "Is there a regulatory or safety constraint that affects what I can recommend here?" Safety-critical contexts demand explicit constraint-checking.
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Forms hypotheses before characterizing the outage pattern
Treats all 14 outages as a uniform set without looking for clustering
Waits for "complete analysis" before recommending any action
Ignores the human/operator dimension entirely
Strong Signals
Characterizes outage pattern before hypothesizing
Uses inflection point to narrow cause family
Recommends interim action while full analysis runs
Frames output as ongoing monitoring, not a one-time report

Product Analytical Strategic
The AI Deployment That Isn't Working
"A financial services client deployed an AI-powered loan underwriting model 3 months ago. Approval rates are similar to before, but loan default rates in the new cohort are running 40% higher than historical benchmarks. You have: application ID, approval decision (AI vs override), applicant features (anonymized), loan amount, and 90-day payment status. What happened and what do you do?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Separate model failure from deployment failure. A model can be technically correct but deployed wrongly (wrong threshold, wrong population, stale training data). These are different problems requiring different fixes. Don't assume the model itself is wrong.
2
Compare AI decisions vs human overrides. If defaults are concentrated in AI-approved loans and human-override loans are performing normally, the model is the problem. If both are defaulting at similar rates, the issue is population shift — the applicant mix changed, not the model.
3
Test for distribution shift. Do the applicant features of the 3-month cohort match the training population? If the model was trained on pre-pandemic data and the applicant pool is now materially different in income, employment type, or debt profile, the model is extrapolating outside its valid range.
4
Check the approval threshold. Was the model's decision threshold set correctly for business risk tolerance? A model trained to minimize false negatives (missed good loans) will inherently approve more risky applications. The threshold is a business decision, not just a technical one.
5
Recommend with urgency and specificity. If the model is causing measurable financial harm, the recommendation is to immediately revert to manual underwriting or tighten the threshold while investigation runs — not to "study further." Frame the cost of inaction explicitly.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
When was the model trained, and on what data? What was the approval threshold set at and why? Are human overrides available as a comparison group? Has the applicant population mix changed? What's the financial cost of each default? Is there regulatory exposure on model decisions?
Check-in Cadence
After model vs deployment distinction: "Is there a technical team who built the model I should loop in, or is this purely a business-side diagnosis?" — Before recommendation: "Is reverting to manual underwriting operationally feasible, or is there a staffing constraint I need to account for?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Assumes the model is wrong without checking the override comparison
Doesn't raise population/distribution shift as a hypothesis
Recommends "retrain the model" without diagnosing what changed
Doesn't treat this as urgent — 40% higher defaults is an active financial crisis
Strong Signals
Immediately separates model failure from deployment failure
Uses AI vs override as a natural control group
Raises distribution shift and threshold calibration as distinct hypotheses
Recommends interim action before full diagnosis is complete

Operations Product
The City Emergency Response Problem
"A metropolitan government wants to reduce ambulance response times, currently averaging 11.4 minutes against a 9-minute target. You have 2 years of dispatch records: incident ID, call timestamp, dispatch timestamp, arrival timestamp, incident type, location zone, unit ID, and unit base location. Where do you start and what would you build?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Decompose the 11.4-minute average into stages. Call → dispatch (call-handling time), dispatch → departure (unit readiness time), departure → arrival (travel time). Each stage has a different intervention. The stage absorbing the most time is the leverage point.
2
Segment by zone, time of day, and incident type. Average response time is almost never uniform. Some zones are chronically underserved. Some incident types (e.g., cardiac vs minor injury) may have different dispatch priorities. Some hours have unit shortages. The segments reveal where the 2.4-minute gap actually lives.
3
Model unit positioning as the primary lever. Travel time is the most controllable variable. With 2 years of incident location data, you can identify demand hotspots by hour and zone — and build a positioning recommendation that pre-positions units where demand is predicted, not where they're parked by default.
4
Scope the Foundry deliverable explicitly. V1 (week 1–2): historical demand heatmap by zone × hour, current unit positioning vs demand gaps. V2: real-time repositioning recommendations dashboard. V3: predictive dispatch queue. Start with the map — it's immediately actionable without any integration work.
5
Name the stakeholder complexity. This is a government engagement. Changes to unit positioning require buy-in from union contracts, fire/EMS leadership, and elected officials. Technical correctness alone doesn't get implementation. A strong candidate raises this without being asked.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Which stage of the response chain is longest on average? Are there zones that systematically exceed target? How many units are available and where are they based? Are there union or contractual constraints on unit repositioning? Is this a real-time system or a planning tool? What does success look like — average response, or worst-decile?
Check-in Cadence
After stage decomposition: "Which of these stages does the department believe is the primary bottleneck?" — After positioning analysis: "Are there contractual or operational constraints on unit repositioning I should factor into the recommendation?" Public sector cases always have non-data constraints.
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Treats 11.4 minutes as a single metric without stage decomposition
Jumps to "hire more units" without analyzing positioning efficiency
Ignores the stakeholder/political complexity of public sector
Proposes real-time system as V1 without scoping to what's feasible first
Strong Signals
Decomposes into call, dispatch, and travel stages immediately
Identifies geographic/temporal segmentation as the key cut
Positions unit repositioning as higher leverage than adding units
Proactively raises stakeholder constraints without being prompted

Analytical Operations
The Manufacturing Yield Problem
"A factory producing precision components is seeing quality yield drop from 94% to 81% over 8 weeks. Each component has a record: component ID, production line, shift, machine ID, operator ID, material batch ID, temperature log, and pass/fail QC result. You have this week to find the cause."
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Apply the 4M lens: Machine, Material, Man, Method. Every manufacturing quality problem traces to one of these. The data gives you all four: machine_id (machine), material_batch_id (material), operator_id (man), production_line × shift (method/environment). Run each as a parallel hypothesis track.
2
Look for concentration first, causation second. Which machine_id, batch_id, shift, or operator_id appears in a disproportionate share of failures? If one machine accounts for 60% of failures at 13% of volume, that's your Pareto cause. Find the concentration before building any causal story.
3
Use the 8-week timeline as a diagnostic anchor. Did the decline start gradually or sharply? A sharp drop 6 weeks ago correlates with a discrete change — a new material batch, a machine service, an operator change, a shift schedule update. A gradual drift suggests cumulative wear or slow process drift.
4
Cross the temperature log against failure rate. Process temperature is a leading indicator in precision manufacturing. If temperature variance correlates with failure rate, you have an environmental/calibration cause that may cut across multiple machines and operators.
5
Recommend a containment action alongside the root cause investigation. While diagnosis runs, identify the highest-failure production line or shift and pause or increase QC frequency there. Don't let 13% defect rate ship while the analysis takes a week.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Is the failure rate uniform across all lines or concentrated? Did anything change operationally 8 weeks ago? Are material batches traceable to a single supplier? What type of failure is QC catching — dimensional, surface, structural? Is the temperature log automated or manually recorded? What's the cost per failed component vs cost of a recall?
Check-in Cadence
After 4M framing: "Does the plant manager have a working theory about cause already? I want to make sure I'm not re-investigating what the team already knows." — After concentration analysis: "Are there any batches currently in production that I should flag for immediate hold while we complete the analysis?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Doesn't use the 4M lens or equivalent multi-cause structure
Builds causal story before finding concentration
Ignores the temperature log entirely
No containment recommendation while analysis runs
Strong Signals
Applies 4M structure to map hypotheses to data columns
Finds Pareto concentration before building causal story
Uses 8-week timeline to narrow cause to discrete vs cumulative
Recommends containment action in parallel with root cause work

Strategic Analytical
The New Market Entry With Bad Data
"A healthcare client wants to expand a telehealth service into three new states. They have patient data from two existing states: patient ID, state, visit type, visit duration, diagnosis code, payer type (insurance vs self-pay), and net revenue per visit. The data is 18 months old and incomplete in one state. Should they expand, and how would you use this data to inform the decision?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Assess data fitness before using it. 18-month-old healthcare data is potentially stale — payer mix, reimbursement rates, and telehealth regulation all shift meaningfully over 18 months. The first question is: is this data reliable enough to base a multi-state expansion on? If not, what's the cost of getting better data vs the cost of the delay?
2
Extract what the data can and can't tell you. It can tell you: which visit types are most profitable, which payer mix yields the best margins, and which diagnosis codes drive volume. It can't tell you: whether the new states' populations match this payer/diagnosis profile, or whether local competition is different.
3
Build the market attractiveness case from what you have. Revenue per visit by payer type × visit type gives you a margin model. If you can estimate the addressable population in the new states (from public health data), you can sketch a revenue projection with explicit uncertainty ranges — not false precision.
4
Propose a sequenced expansion, not a simultaneous one. Enter the one state where the data is cleanest and population profile is most similar to existing markets. Use it as a live test. Expand to the other two once the model is validated in-market. This reduces decision risk without blocking expansion entirely.
5
Name the data gaps that would change your recommendation. "If the new states have a significantly higher self-pay population, our margin model breaks because self-pay revenue per visit is X% lower. That's the key variable I'd want to validate before committing to all three states simultaneously."
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
Why is the data 18 months old — is updated data available? What's the competitive telehealth landscape in the new states? Are there regulatory differences in telehealth licensure by state? What's the capital commitment required per state entry? What does incomplete mean in the one state — missing records or missing fields? Is there a deadline forcing the expansion decision?
Check-in Cadence
After data fitness assessment: "Before I model the expansion, I want to flag that 18-month-old data in healthcare may have material limitations — is there a reason we're working with this vintage rather than more recent data?" — Before recommendation: "Is there a board deadline or capital raise that constrains the timeline for this decision?"
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Uses the data at face value without questioning its age or completeness
Gives a binary yes/no without acknowledging uncertainty
Recommends entering all three states simultaneously
Doesn't name which data gap would change the recommendation
Strong Signals
Questions data fitness before building the model
Explicitly separates what the data can and can't tell you
Proposes sequenced entry to reduce risk
Names the specific variable that would break the recommendation

The Internal Tool Nobody Adopted
"Your firm deployed a dashboard for a manufacturing client's procurement team 6 weeks ago. Expected 80% of the 45-person team to be weekly active users within 30 days. Actual: 9 users, mostly senior analysts. You have usage logs: user ID, department, seniority level, login count, features accessed, and session duration. The client is asking whether the project was a failure. How do you respond?"
Ideal Logic Flow
1
Separate product failure from change management failure. A tool can work perfectly and still not get adopted. If the 9 users are getting real value (long sessions, all features accessed, repeat logins), the product is fine — the rollout process failed. These require completely different responses to the client.
2
Profile the 9 adopters vs the 36 non-adopters. Are adopters senior analysts because they're the only ones who were trained? Or because the tool genuinely only serves that persona? If the tool was designed for analysts but sold to the whole procurement team, the scope was misaligned from the start.
3
Don't accept the framing that it's a failure yet. 6 weeks is early for enterprise tool adoption. The question to the client is: what was the evidence basis for the 80% / 30-day target? If it was aspirational rather than benchmarked, the target was wrong, not the product.
4
Present a recovery plan, not a post-mortem. Instead of defending or conceding, immediately propose: targeted training sessions for the non-adopter cohort, a 30-day re-engagement campaign, and a revised adoption target with a clear milestone. Take ownership of the outcome, not just the delivery.
5
Use your firm's credibility as a lever. The client chose you because of delivery quality. Frame this as: "Our job isn't done at deployment — it's done when your team is getting value. Here's what we're going to do in the next 30 days to get there." This is the ownership mindset the hiring framework specifically evaluates.
Questions the Candidate Should Ask
What training or onboarding was provided at launch? Are the 9 adopters getting measurable value from the tool? Was the 80% target agreed in the contract or internal? What are non-adopters using instead — Excel, email, nothing? Is there a manager or champion within the procurement team? What's the client's patience timeline before escalating?
Check-in Cadence
This case tests emotional intelligence as much as analytical skill. Strong probe: "How would you handle this conversation if the client is visibly frustrated?" and "Would you involve your manager before responding, or handle it directly?" — Both answers reveal ownership vs escalation instincts.
Evaluation Signals
Red Flags
Immediately concedes it's a failure to appease the client
Defends the delivery without diagnosing adoption failure type
No recovery plan — only analysis of what went wrong
Doesn't distinguish product failure from change management failure
Strong Signals
Separates product quality from change management failure
Profiles adopters vs non-adopters before accepting the failure framing
Questions the 80% target's basis before defending against it
Leads with a recovery plan and takes explicit ownership of the outcome
